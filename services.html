ColorStack

At ColorStack, our core mission is to dismantle barriers to representation in the tech industry. Conceived by Jehron Petty, who keenly observed the underrepresentation of Black and Latinx Computer Science students at Cornell, our organization is passionately committed to amplifying the presence of Black and Latinx CS graduates in the realm of rewarding technical careers. Rooted in our unwavering dedication to diversity and inclusion, we paint a vision of a future where Black and Latinx technologists lead the charge in driving innovation. Personally, this journey has allowed me to connect with a vibrant community, fostering countless valuable relationships and providing opportunities to meet potential mentors who have profoundly influenced my career path. Within our network, a spirit of mutual support thrives, where shared advice becomes a guiding force propelling each of us forward in our professional journeys. It's a collaborative ecosystem where collective wisdom propels us toward success, reinforcing our commitment to creating a tech landscape that truly reflects the richness of human diversity.

SHPE

At the Texas A&M University Chapter of SHPE, they are a close-knit community of Hispanic engineers, where we particularly are engage in bringing career professionalism to Hispanics. In the #SHPEfamilia, we prioritize personal and professional growth within the Aggie spirit. Nationally, SHPE aims to empower the Hispanic community in STEM fields, envisioning a world where Hispanics are influential leaders in innovation. Join us in creating a future where diversity is celebrated, and with the guidance and mentorship of SHPE, every individual, including myself, has the support to excel in their academic and career journey.

TAMU FIRST-GENERATION ENGINEERING STUDENTS MENTORING PROGRAM

The primary goal of the FGEn Mentoring Program is to enhance Texas A&M first-generation engineering students educational and social experiences on campus. It is about leveraging university and community resources to provide a sense of community to FGEn students, and help them with their personal and professional goals. If neither of your parents have earned a four-year degree, you are a first-generation student. The Texas A&M University College of Engineering is home to more than 20% first-generation undergraduate students.
